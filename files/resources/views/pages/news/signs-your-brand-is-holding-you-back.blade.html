Nobody books a call to say their brand is broken. They book a call because the deals are stalling, the deck feels embarrassing to send, and the site does not sound like the company they actually built. Here is what that usually means.

1. You explain what you do twice

Once with the words on the site, then again on the call — properly this time. If the second explanation is always the good one, the site is not doing its job. The fix is rarely more copy. It is deciding which single sentence you are willing to lead with, and cutting the four that hedge it.

2. Your best customers found you anyway

Referrals are wonderful and they hide everything. A brand that only works when a trusted human vouches for it first is not a brand, it is a warm introduction with a logo attached. The test is what happens to a stranger who lands cold at 11pm.

If the deal only closes after you get on the call, your brand is a bottleneck wearing a nice typeface.

3. The deck and the site disagree

Different headline, different colours, different promise. This happens because the deck gets updated for every pitch and the site gets updated never. It reads to a buyer as a company still arguing with itself — and buyers are extremely good at noticing that.

4. Every new page is a small crisis

If shipping a pricing page needs a designer, a debate, and two weeks, you do not have a system — you have a set of one-off artefacts. A real brand system makes the twentieth asset as easy as the first:

  • Type and spacing decided once, not re-litigated per page
  • Components your engineers can reach for without asking
  • Rules written down in the place the work actually happens

5. You have stopped looking at it

The most reliable signal of all. When a founder will not open their own homepage in a meeting, they already know. The embarrassment is information — it is just information nobody wants to write down.

So what now?

None of these are design problems, exactly. They are decision problems that show up as design. That is why we start every engagement with a week of discovery and hard questions rather than a mood board: the brand is the output, but the decisions are the work.
